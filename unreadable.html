Why Leaves Change Color

A short, readable guide for curious observers

Science in brief

Leaves are green during the growing season because of chlorophyll. As days shorten and temperatures fall, trees reclaim nutrients and chlorophyll breaks down. Other pigments—carotenoids (yellows/oranges), anthocyanins (reds/purples), and tannins (browns)—become visible or are produced, creating autumn color.

Key factors

  • Daylength: Shorter days trigger the seasonal changes.
  • Temperature: Cool nights and sunny days favor bright reds and clear golds.
  • Moisture: Adequate (but not extreme) moisture helps; drought can dull colors.
  • Species & health: Different trees show different colors; stressed trees may color early or faintly.

Simple timeline

  • Late summer: chlorophyll breakdown begins.
  • Early-mid autumn: pigments peak in many regions.
  • Late autumn: pigments fade and leaves fall.

What's in a red?

Anthocyanins form in some species when sugars become trapped in leaves under sunny days and cool nights. They may protect leaf tissues and can intensify red shades.

Tips for a walk

Bring a notebook: note species, temperature, and sky. Compare sunlit and shaded sides of trees—reds usually intensify in direct light. Hilltops and lake margins often change first.

“It is not so much that the leaves change as that the trees practice letting go.”

Quick reference

  • Primary pigments: chlorophyll, carotenoids, anthocyanins, tannins
  • Best viewing: sunny days + cool, frost-free nights
  • Typical peak: varies by region (often mid-late autumn)
